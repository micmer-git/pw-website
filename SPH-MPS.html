Particle Methods Deep-Dive

FVM vs SPH vs MPS
The comparison that matters for industrial CFD

Traditional Finite Volume (mesh-based), Smoothed Particle Hydrodynamics (compressible particle-based) and Moving Particle Semi-implicit (incompressible particle-based) — three approaches, very different trade-offs. Here's the technical breakdown with the numbers.

TL;DR — why MPS wins for industrial fluid applications

For incompressible free-surface flows — oil jets, gearbox churning, mixing, sloshing, coolant splashing — MPS offers ~10–30× larger time steps than SPH (no sound-speed CFL), no meshing overhead like FVM, and stable multi-physics coupling. The result: simulation turnaround measured in hours, not weeks.

Three Methods, Three Trade-offs

Which CFD method fits your problem?

FVM dominates classical aerodynamics. SPH leads large-deformation compressible flow. MPS is purpose-built for incompressible free-surface industrial fluid dynamics.

The bottom line

Why MPS is the preferred method at Particleworks

For the applications that matter to industrial engineers — lubrication, cooling, sloshing, mixing, splashing — MPS is not a trade-off. It's a structural win.

Bigger time-step, by design

The semi-implicit pressure Poisson solve removes the sound-speed CFL barrier that caps SPH. Water at 5 m/s with 1 mm resolution: SPH runs at Δt ≈ 2×10⁻⁶ s, MPS at Δt ≈ 5×10⁻⁴ s.

≈ 250× larger Δt

No meshing, ever

The meshing step that dominates FVM workflows — adaptive refinement near walls, periodic remesh on moving parts — simply does not exist. Just seed particles in the fluid domain and run.

CAD → run: < 10 min

Smooth pressure, stable physics

Poisson-based pressure avoids the noisy fields of weakly-compressible SPH. Heat transfer, DEM coupling, rheology and phase change all plug in without the stability gymnastics SPH requires.

Multi-physics native

Free-surface for free

The interface between oil and air emerges from the particle distribution itself — no VOF, no level-set, no interface smearing. Perfect for sprays, jets, splashing and churning.

Sharp, diffusion-free

Rotating & moving parts

Gears, pistons, impellers, clutches — geometries that break mesh-based CFD move through particle fields without any topology change. Rigid-body coupling is native, not a plug-in.

Native 6-DOF

Industrial validation

Adopted by Ducati Corse (racing piston cooling), Hyundai (churning losses), Ricardo (e-motor thermal), TotalEnergies (spray cooling), GKN Driveline, Wärtsilä, Royal Enfield. Not a niche method — the industrial standard for incompressible particle CFD.

Under the hood

The one equation that changes everything

SPH and MPS start from the same Navier-Stokes. They diverge on how pressure is obtained — and that single choice cascades into the Δt, stability and runtime differences above.

SPH — explicit

Equation of state, weakly compressible

p = B [ (ρ / ρ₀)γ − 1 ]   (Tait)
Δt < 0.25 · h / c   (sound-speed CFL)
c ≈ 10 · Umax   (Mach < 0.1)

Pressure is computed instantaneously from a local density deviation. Cheap per step — but Δt is capped by the artificial speed of sound.

MPS — implicit

Pressure Poisson, incompressible

∇²pn+1 = (ρ / Δt) · ∇·u*
un+1 = u* − (Δt/ρ) · ∇pn+1
Δt < C · h / Umax  (velocity CFL only)

A linear system is solved each step — more expensive per iteration. But Δt is bounded only by the physical velocity, so orders of magnitude fewer steps are needed.
