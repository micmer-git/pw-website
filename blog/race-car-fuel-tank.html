Optimizing Race Car Fuel Tank Design: A Meshless CFD Approach

Michele Merelli March 2025 7 min read

Fuel Tanks Are Not Boring

In professional motorsport, the fuel tank is not a passive container. It is a dynamic system operating under extreme lateral, longitudinal and vertical accelerations that would make most engineering simulations fall apart. A poorly designed tank costs you lap time in three different ways: it shifts the centre of gravity at the worst possible moment, it starves the feed pump during hard cornering, and it creates sloshing forces that destabilise the chassis mid-corner.

Dallara Automobili, the Italian constructor behind chassis for IndyCar, Formula 2, Formula 3, and a long list of GT and prototype programmes, recently published a breakthrough study showing how meshless CFD fundamentally changes what is possible in fuel tank design iteration.

The Engineering Constraints

Race car fuel tanks must satisfy a set of requirements that directly conflict with each other:

  • A higher centre of gravity increases lateral load transfer during cornering, degrading mechanical grip
  • Feed pumps must maintain consistent fuel delivery under sustained 2g+ lateral and braking loads
  • Violent sloshing can momentarily uncover the pickup, causing engine hesitation or outright starvation
  • Internal baffles add weight and reduce usable volume — the opposite of what you want

The traditional workflow uses FVM-based VOF simulations. These work, but they are computationally savage. Dallara's benchmarks tell the story clearly.

Particle Size Sensitivity

Any simulation engineer will immediately ask about mesh independence — or in this case, particle size independence. Dallara ran a systematic convergence study and the results are reassuring. As particle size increased from 2.5 mm to 4.0 mm, the maximum error in sloshing force prediction grew from 0.9% to 2.1%. The 3.5 mm particle size provided the best balance between accuracy and computational cost, keeping errors well below engineering tolerances while maintaining fast turnaround.

The PTFE Sphere Innovation

The idea: Fill partially empty fuel tanks with loose Teflon (PTFE) spheres that float on the fuel surface, suppressing free-surface oscillation and lowering the effective centre of gravity of the fuel mass.

This is not an obvious solution. PTFE is chemically inert, lightweight, and survives the thermal and chemical environment inside a fuel cell. But predicting how thousands of loose spheres interact with sloshing fuel under multi-axis acceleration is exactly the kind of problem that defeats traditional mesh-based approaches. With MPS, Dallara could model the coupled fluid-sphere dynamics directly.

The results were striking. The addition of PTFE spheres lowered the vertical centre of gravity (Z-COG) of the fuel mass by 17%. Sloshing amplitudes dropped significantly, meaning more consistent fuel delivery and a calmer chassis balance through transient manoeuvres.

For a constructor competing in formulae where hundredths of a second define the grid, a 17% COG reduction in the fuel system is not incremental. It is a genuine competitive advantage that would have been nearly impossible to quantify without particle-based simulation.
