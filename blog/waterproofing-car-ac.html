Waterproofing Car AC Air Intakes: CFD Simulations Prevent Water Intrusion

Michele Merelli June 2024 5 min read

A Problem Hiding in Plain Sight

Every car on the road pulls fresh air into the cabin through vents located in the cowl area — that narrow trough between the base of the windshield and the trailing edge of the hood. It is a convenient location aerodynamically, but it is also exactly where rainwater concentrates. Windshield runoff, hood runoff, and direct rainfall all converge in this zone, and the only thing standing between that water and the passenger cabin is a set of baffles and drain channels designed decades ago.

When waterproofing fails, the consequences range from annoying to dangerous: fogged windows that reduce visibility, musty smells from trapped moisture breeding mould in the HVAC ducting, and in severe cases, water dripping onto electronics or directly onto the driver's feet. OEMs take this seriously — waterproofing tests are a standard part of vehicle validation — but finding and fixing infiltration paths late in the programme is expensive and disruptive.

Why This Problem Suits Particle Simulation

Rain hitting a car is not a steady-state, single-phase problem. It involves droplets of varying size impacting surfaces at different angles, forming thin films that flow under gravity and aerodynamic pressure, accumulating in pockets, and occasionally overflowing into places they should not reach. Traditional VOF methods can handle this, but the mesh refinement required to capture thin films and small overflow events makes the models large, slow, and fragile.

Particleworks treats every rain droplet and every film fragment as a collection of particles governed by the MPS method. There is no mesh to break when water films thin to sub-millimetre thickness. There is no special treatment needed when a stream of water separates from a surface and becomes airborne droplets. The physics just works.

Diagnosing the Baseline Design

Engineers set up the simulation with the vehicle at a 15-degree uphill angle — a worst-case scenario that directs maximum water flow toward the cowl intake. The simulation revealed two distinct infiltration mechanisms that would have been difficult to separate in a physical rain test:

  • Direct droplet entry: Rain falling at certain angles passed straight through the intake opening without contacting any baffle surface
  • Overflow accumulation: Water pooling in the cowl trough eventually exceeded the drain capacity and spilled over the intake lip into the HVAC duct

Understanding these two mechanisms separately is critical because they require different countermeasures. A fix that blocks overflow will do nothing against direct droplets, and vice versa.

Two Countermeasures, Two Lessons

Countermeasure 1
Bottom-Edge Barrier Wall

A simple raised wall along the bottom edge of the intake opening, designed to contain pooled water and redirect it toward the drain channels.

Overflow reduced by 90% — but direct droplets unaffected
Countermeasure 2
Overhanging Cover (Range Hood Concept)

An overhanging shroud inspired by kitchen range hood ducting, creating a downward-facing entry that forces incoming air (and water) to change direction before reaching the intake. Droplets cannot follow the sharp turn; the air can.

Total water infiltration reduced by ~90% versus original design

The second countermeasure is the elegant one. By borrowing a concept from an entirely different engineering domain — kitchen ventilation — the team created a geometry that passively separates water from air using nothing more than inertia. The heavier water droplets cannot follow the sharp 180-degree turn into the intake; the lighter air flows through easily. No moving parts, no additional drains, no maintenance.

The simulation made it possible to test both concepts in days rather than weeks of physical prototyping, and to understand precisely why one worked better than the other. That insight feeds forward into design guidelines for future vehicle programmes.
