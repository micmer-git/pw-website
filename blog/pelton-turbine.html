Simulation of a Pelton Turbine: Application to Two Challenging Situations

Michele Merelli September 2024 9 min read

The Pelton Problem

Pelton turbines convert the kinetic energy of high-velocity water jets into mechanical rotation. They are the workhorses of high-head hydropower installations, and their design has been refined over more than a century. Yet two phenomena continue to frustrate engineers and operators: the falaise effect — a sharp, sudden efficiency drop when jet velocity decreases below a critical threshold — and splitter erosion, where the thin central ridge dividing each bucket gradually degrades under the relentless impact of water and entrained sediment.

Both problems are inherently transient, three-dimensional, and involve complex free-surface fragmentation. Traditional volume-based CFD can address them, but at extraordinary computational cost. A peer-reviewed paper by Jean Decaix, Mathieu Mettille, and Cécile Münch-Alligné from HES-SO Valais-Wallis explores whether the MPS particle method in Particleworks can do it faster and better.

Simulation Setup

The researchers modelled a laboratory-scale Pelton turbine with the following configuration:

  • Particle diameter: 2.5 mm
  • Total particle count: approximately 12 million
  • Hardware: a single NVIDIA Quadro RTX 5000 GPU
  • Multiple operating points spanning the full efficiency curve, including the critical low-speed region where the falaise appears

The choice of a 2.5 mm particle size was driven by a convergence study showing it provided sufficient resolution to capture the bucket filling, sheet formation, and drainage processes that govern torque generation. Larger particles missed the thin water sheets leaving the bucket trailing edge; smaller particles drove up the count without meaningful accuracy gains.

The FVM simulation required 10 days on a 24-core machine and could not even complete a single revolution. The MPS simulation delivered two full revolutions in 24 hours on one GPU. This is not an academic curiosity — it is the difference between running one design iteration per sprint and running ten.

Capturing the Falaise Effect

The falaise (French for "cliff") describes the precipitous efficiency drop that Pelton turbines experience when operated significantly below their design speed. As the runner slows, the relative velocity between the incoming jet and the bucket changes, altering the flow pattern inside the bucket. Water no longer drains cleanly from the trailing edge; instead, it accumulates and interferes with neighbouring buckets. The result is vibration, noise, and a dramatic loss of power.

The MPS simulation captured this transition clearly. At nominal speed, the water sheet exits the bucket in a well-defined pattern with minimal interference. As speed decreases, the simulation showed increasing water retention, bucket-to-bucket interference, and the characteristic sharp efficiency cliff that operators observe in the field. The ability to visualise and quantify this transient process gives turbine designers direct insight into where the falaise begins and what geometric modifications might delay it.

Predicting Splitter Erosion

The splitter — the sharp ridge at the centre of each Pelton bucket — takes the full force of the incoming jet. Over thousands of hours of operation, this impact erodes the splitter, rounding its edge and reducing the turbine's ability to split the jet symmetrically. Asymmetric bucket filling means asymmetric torque, vibration, and progressive efficiency loss.

Erosion prediction demands accurate knowledge of impact velocity, angle, and the volume of water striking the splitter surface at each instant. The MPS approach tracks every particle impact directly, providing a natural framework for erosion modelling without the interpolation artefacts that plague mesh-based approaches at thin geometric features.

The MPS erosion prediction showed less than 0.2% deviation from the theoretical erosion rate, compared to 0.7% for the FVM approach. For a turbine operator planning maintenance intervals worth millions of euros, that threefold improvement in prediction accuracy translates directly into better asset management.
