Unleashing the Potential: How Particleworks CFD Cracked the E-Motor Cooling Challenge

Michele Merelli June 2024 8 min read

The Cooling Problem Nobody Wants to Talk About

As electric vehicle adoption accelerates, the pressure on traction motor design is relentless: more power from smaller packages, tighter thermal margins, and absolute reliability over hundreds of thousands of kilometres. The cooling system is where all of this converges. Get it wrong and you face demagnetisation, winding insulation failure, or premature bearing degradation. Get it right and you unlock power density that the competition cannot match.

Traditional CFD tools can handle single-phase flows through jackets and channels well enough. But the moment you introduce oil jets impinging on copper windings, splashing through narrow air gaps, and draining back under gravity, the physics become genuinely difficult. Volume-based meshing chokes on the free-surface fragmentation. Run times balloon. Engineers end up simplifying geometry until the simulation barely resembles what sits on the test bench.

This is exactly where the particle-based approach changes the game.

Why Particles Beat Meshes for Oil-Jet Cooling

The MPS (Moving Particle Semi-implicit) method treats the fluid as discrete particles rather than carving the domain into millions of tiny cells. There is no mesh to generate, no mesh to deform, and no mesh to repair when oil breaks into thin films and droplets against a hot stator. The particles simply follow Newtonian mechanics and interact through kernel functions that enforce conservation of mass and momentum.

For an oil-cooled e-motor, this translates to a practical advantage: you can model the full complexity of jet impingement, film flow, and gravity drainage without the weeks of mesh preparation that a traditional Eulerian approach would require. And because GPU-accelerated MPS is inherently parallel, a single high-end graphics card can replace a multi-node cluster.

Inside the Simulation: A Three-Stage Strategy

The e-motor geometry featured a central shaft feeding cooling oil outward through strategically placed jets aimed at the stator windings and end regions. Rather than brute-forcing the entire two-phase problem in a single massive run, the engineering team designed a layered workflow that exploits the physics at each scale.

Stage 1 — Oil Flow Distribution

An internal flow simulation mapped how the total oil supply splits between the three jet groups. The result was a 47 / 34 / 19 percent distribution ratio across the groups. This single finding already has enormous design value: it tells you whether each winding zone receives enough coolant, and it identifies where to enlarge or restrict orifices if it does not.

Stage 2 — Air Field Mapping

The oil flow field informed a follow-on air flow simulation using larger 0.5 mm particles — roughly sixteen times fewer than a full two-phase model at oil-resolution. This step captures the recirculating air patterns inside the motor housing that influence how oil films spread and how droplets are entrained. Running it separately kept the computational cost manageable without sacrificing the coupled physics.

Stage 3 — High-Resolution Impingement

With the air flow mapped, a final high-resolution 0.2 mm particle simulation captured the complex oil jet impingement patterns and the intricate wetting of the motor components. This is where the real engineering insight lives: you can see exactly which surfaces are flooded, which are starved, and how the heat transfer coefficient (HTC) varies across the geometry.

The entire oil flow simulation required just 6 days on a single GPU despite modelling millions of particles. Compare that to the typical multi-week timeline and multi-node cluster requirement for a comparable volume-of-fluid approach, and the efficiency advantage becomes hard to ignore.

Closing the Loop: CFD Meets Finite Element Thermal Analysis

A beautiful flow visualisation means nothing if it cannot predict the temperatures an engineer will measure on a real motor. To get there, the CFD-derived HTC distributions were exported and mapped onto a finite element (FE) thermal model of the complete motor assembly — stator laminations, winding bundles, insulation layers, housing, and bearings.

The coupled simulation was then compared against experimental thermocouple measurements at six winding locations distributed around the stator. The result: predictions matched within ±2.8 °C across every measurement point. That is an exceptional level of accuracy for a system with this many interacting thermal paths, and it validates the entire simulation chain from particle flow to structural temperature.

What This Means for Your Next Motor Programme

The days of over-engineering cooling systems with generous safety margins are ending. Battery capacity is precious, motor envelopes are shrinking, and every watt of pumping power that goes to moving coolant is a watt that does not reach the wheels. Accurate, efficient simulation of oil jet cooling is no longer optional — it is the difference between a competitive powertrain and an also-ran.

Key Takeaway 01

Particle-based CFD efficiently simulates complex multiphase e-motor cooling flows without the mesh generation bottleneck that stalls traditional approaches.

Key Takeaway 02

CFD modelling revealed the 47/34/19% flow split ratio and detailed jet impingement patterns, providing actionable data for cooling circuit optimisation.

Key Takeaway 03

The coupled CFD-FEA workflow validated winding temperatures within ±2.8 °C of experimental measurements across all six probe locations.
